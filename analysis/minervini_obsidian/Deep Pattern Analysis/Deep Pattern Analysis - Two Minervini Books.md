## Core Pattern Sequence

The repeated two-book sequence is:

1. **Candidate selection:** price already behaves like a leader, often before the story feels comfortable.
2. **Trend qualification:** the stock is in or entering stage 2, with price above important moving averages and the averages aligned or improving.
3. **Base construction:** the stock pauses without giving back its leadership character.
4. **Contraction:** each pullback should become less damaging than the previous one; volatility narrows.
5. **Supply dry-up:** volume should become quieter near the final tight area, showing fewer sellers.
6. **Pivot definition:** the buy point is planned before entry, not discovered after chasing.
7. **Entry execution:** buy as price moves through the planned area with confirmation, while risk remains close.
8. **Failure handling:** if the breakout fails, the setup changes category from buy lesson to sell/avoid lesson.

## How VCP And Contraction Appear

VCP is not merely a triangle or a pretty base. In these images, it appears as a behavioral sequence:

- The first pullback proves the stock can correct without destroying the larger advance.
- The second pullback should be shallower, faster, or less volatile.
- The final pullback should often be tight enough that a pivot becomes obvious.
- Volume should become less threatening near the final contraction.
- The best entry is near the end of contraction, not after price has already traveled far from the pivot.

Practical read: when the final contraction is still wide, the trade may work but the stop becomes too far away. When the final contraction is narrow, the entry can be planned with smaller risk and better reward/risk.

## How Entries Are Planned

An entry is planned in layers:

1. **Context gate:** Is the stock a leader or only bouncing?
2. **Trend gate:** Does it qualify as stage 2 or close to stage 2?
3. **Structure gate:** Is there a base, pullback, shelf, handle, or VCP-like tightening area?
4. **Supply gate:** Is volume drying up into the quiet area?
5. **Pivot gate:** Where exactly is the price that proves demand is taking control?
6. **Risk gate:** If wrong, where is the exit? If that stop is too far, the entry is rejected or position size is reduced.
7. **Confirmation gate:** Does price move through the pivot with enough force, or does it stall and reverse?

The two books keep returning to the same principle: do not separate the buy point from the stop. A breakout that requires a huge stop is not a clean Minervini-style entry, even if the chart later goes up.

## IPO / New Issue Pattern

The IPO lesson is not "new stock equals buy." The useful pattern is:

- the issue lists,
- early volatility is absorbed,
- price stops reacting badly to supply,
- a first base or tight shelf forms,
- volume quiets down near the pivot,
- the breakout offers a nearby invalidation point.

If a new issue immediately loses its opening range, repeatedly breaks support, or becomes too extended before forming a proper base, it is not a clean entry. It becomes either a watchlist item or a failure lesson.

## Fundamentals And Catalysts

The image layer teaches that fundamentals should explain and support leadership, not replace the chart. Across the chapter arc:

- sales and earnings acceleration help explain why institutions may accumulate,
- industry group strength increases the odds that the move is not isolated,
- catalysts can attract sponsorship,
- earnings quality protects against low-quality surprises,
- but the actual entry still requires price/volume confirmation.

In practice: fundamentals choose the pond; the chart chooses the exact stone to step on.

## Failure And Sell Pattern

Failure images are not secondary. They teach the boundary of the setup:

- A breakout that cannot hold the pivot is no longer a breakout lesson.
- Heavy volume on downside reversal is supply, not noise.
- A stock that violates the base low after entry has changed character.
- Climax/exhaustion appears after a major advance, when price becomes too obvious and too vertical.
- The sell decision should be tied to the prewritten plan, not to hope that the chart will fix itself.

## Final Operating Model

Use this order when studying any of the book images:

1. Name the pattern: VCP, pivot breakout, cheat entry, IPO base, failed breakout, climax/top, shakeout, volume dry-up, or relative-strength lead.
2. Mark the pivot and invalidation point.
3. Ask whether the final contraction makes the risk small enough.
4. Check whether volume supports the interpretation.
5. Check whether the stock had leadership before the entry.
6. Add fundamentals only after the chart behavior is clear.
7. Decide whether the image teaches buying, waiting, selling, or avoiding.
